Introduction

Why This Exists

You're selling local visibility, lead capture, and AI-powered web presence. To sell it confidently — and deliver it well — you need to own the knowledge behind it.

This curriculum exists so you never have to say "let me look that up" in a client conversation. It starts with what you need right now and grows with you. Every chapter covers one domain: what it is, why it matters, and what you actually do with it.

Add a chapter when you hit a problem you don't fully understand. Update existing sections when you learn something was wrong or incomplete. The goal isn't a perfect textbook — it's a tool that makes you sharper every month.

How to use this

Read sequentially once to build the mental model. Then use the sidebar to jump to whatever's relevant in the moment — before a sales call, a client audit, or a GBP session.

Chapter 1

Local SEO Fundamentals

Local SEO is how a small business shows up when someone nearby searches for what they do. It's different from general SEO — proximity and trust signals matter more than domain authority.

How Local Search Works

When someone searches "concrete contractor Nampa" or "window cleaner near me," Google serves two distinct result types: the Local Pack (map results) and organic blue links. These are ranked by completely different algorithms.

The Local Pack is powered by Google Business Profile data. Organic results are powered by the website's authority and relevance. Most local service businesses should prioritize the Local Pack first — it sits above organic results and gets a disproportionate share of clicks.

NAP Consistency

NAP = Name, Address, Phone. These three fields must be identical everywhere your client appears online — GBP, Yelp, BBB, Angi, their website footer, Facebook, everywhere.

Google cross-references NAP data across the web to confirm a business is real and located where it claims. Inconsistencies — "St." vs "Street," an old phone number, a slightly different business name — create conflicting signals that suppress rankings.

Common NAP mistakes

"Phoenix Stoneworks" vs "Phoenix Stoneworks LLC" — Google may treat these as different entities. Pick one exact form and use it everywhere.

  • Use the exact legal business name consistently — no abbreviations unless they're in the legal name
  • Use a local phone number, not a toll-free number
  • If the business moves or changes its number, update every directory immediately
  • The address on GBP must match the address on the website verbatim

The Local Pack

The map pack — three business listings shown below the map widget — is the highest-value placement for local service businesses. It appears for nearly every service + city search and gets 30–50% of all clicks on the page.

Getting into the pack requires strong GBP signals (Chapter 2), consistent citations (Chapter 3), and a steady review velocity (Chapter 4). There is no shortcut — but for small businesses, it's genuinely achievable in 3–6 months with consistent work.

Local Pack Ranking Factors

Google uses three core factors, in roughly this order of importance:

Relevance

Does your GBP category and profile match what the searcher is looking for? This is the most controllable factor.

Proximity

How close is the searcher to the business? You can't change geography, but service area setup matters.

Prominence

How well-known and trusted is the business? Driven by reviews, citations, backlinks, and GBP activity.

Chapter 2

Google Business Profile

GBP is the single highest-leverage channel for local clients. It's free real estate that most small businesses leave half-built. Your job is to build and maintain it properly.

Categories

The primary category is the most important field on the entire GBP. Google uses it to decide which searches your profile is eligible to appear for. Choose too broadly ("Contractor") and you compete for nothing. Choose precisely ("Concrete Contractor") and you rank for exactly what your client does.

  • Use Google's existing category list — don't invent your own
  • Add secondary categories for each additional service (masonry, stamped concrete, etc.)
  • Review the category list annually — Google adds and renames them

GBP Posts

Posts are treated as fresh content signals. An active profile outranks an identical but dormant one. For Momentum clients, this is part of your monthly deliverable.

Post Type When to Use Frequency
Update News, team updates, service changes Weekly
Offer Seasonal promotions, discounts Monthly
Event Trade shows, open houses, seasonal pushes As needed

Posts expire after 7 days for Update/Offer types. You need to post consistently — not just once.

Photos

Real job photos outperform stock photos by a wide margin. Google tracks photo engagement and profiles with active photo uploads rank higher. For trade clients (concrete, masonry, cleaning), before/after shots are gold.

  • Upload at least 5 photos at onboarding — exterior, team, completed jobs
  • Add 2–4 photos per month as part of Momentum delivery
  • Geotag photos when possible — it adds a location signal
  • Never upload screenshots or watermarked stock images

Q&A

The Q&A section on GBP is user-generated by default — anyone can post a question, and anyone can answer it. Seed your own questions before someone else does and gives a wrong answer.

  • Write 5–8 questions covering price range, service area, hours, booking process
  • Answer them yourself from the owner's account — "Hi, this is [Name]..."
  • Monitor monthly and respond to any new user questions promptly

Monthly GBP Audit Checklist

Run this for every Momentum client each month before writing posts.

Business name, address, phone still accurate
Hours up to date (including holiday hours)
All services listed with descriptions
Website URL pointing to current site
At least 1 new photo uploaded this month
At least 2 new posts published this month
All new reviews have responses
No unanswered Q&A questions
No Google-suggested edits pending review
Chapter 3

Citations

Citations are any online mention of a business's NAP data — directory listings, social profiles, industry sites. They're not as powerful as they used to be, but they still matter for local pack rankings and they catch errors that hurt you.

What Citations Are

A citation doesn't have to be a link — it's any place on the web where the business name, address, and phone appear together. Google aggregates these signals to confirm the business is legitimate and located where it claims.

Think of citations as a vote of existence. More votes from credible sources = more trust. Contradictory votes (wrong address on an old listing) = confusion and suppressed rankings.

Where to Build Citations

Directory Priority Notes
Google Business Profile Critical Chapter 2 — the foundation
Apple Maps Critical Powers Siri + Maps on iOS
Bing Places Critical Free, takes 5 minutes
Yelp High Never pay for ads — just maintain the free listing
BBB High Free listing, paid accreditation not necessary
Facebook Business High Also a social proof signal
Angi / HomeAdvisor Medium Industry-specific — great for contractors
Houzz Medium Good for home improvement / masonry
Nextdoor Medium Neighborhood-level trust signal

Auditing & Fixing Citations

Use Whitespark's Citation Finder or BrightLocal's Citation Audit to find where a client is listed and catch inconsistencies.

For a new client at onboarding, manually check the top 5 directories (GBP, Apple, Bing, Yelp, Facebook) and fix any NAP mismatches before doing anything else. The rest can wait.

Prioritize fixes over new listings

A wrong phone number on 3 major directories does more damage than missing 20 minor ones. Fix first, build second.

Chapter 4

Reviews

Reviews are the most visible trust signal for local businesses — and one of the top ranking factors for the Local Pack. Quantity, recency, and response rate all matter.

Why They Matter

Google weights reviews across three dimensions:

  • Quantity — more reviews than competitors help you rank above them
  • Recency — reviews in the last 90 days are weighted more heavily than older ones; a business that stopped getting reviews in 2023 looks inactive
  • Diversity — reviews that mention different services tell Google what the business does

Conversion-wise: 88% of consumers trust online reviews as much as personal recommendations. A business with 4.8 stars and 40 reviews almost always beats one with 4.9 and 6 reviews.

Getting Reviews

The best time to ask is immediately after a job is completed and the client is happy. Text outperforms email by 3–4x for response rate.

Template: Post-job review ask (SMS)

"Hi [name], it was great working with you on [project]. If you have 2 minutes, a Google review means the world to a small business like ours: [short link]. Thank you!"

  • Create a short review link in GBP under "Get more reviews" — share this, not the full URL
  • Never incentivize reviews — Google's policies prohibit it and it can get the profile suspended
  • Don't ask for 5 stars — ask for "honest feedback"
  • Set a goal of 2–3 new reviews per month at minimum

Responding to Reviews

Every review needs a response — positive and negative. Google treats response rate as a signal of engagement. Unanswered reviews look like an abandoned business.

Review Type Response Goal Tone
5-star, no comment Thank them, mention a service Warm, brief
5-star, detailed Thank them personally, reflect their words Genuine, specific
3–4 star Acknowledge, take offline Calm, professional
1–2 star De-escalate, offer resolution, keep it brief Empathetic, never defensive
Negative review rule

Never argue publicly. One sentence acknowledging the experience + one sentence offering to make it right + contact info. That's it. Future readers will judge your response more than the complaint.

Chapter 5

On-Page Local SEO

What's on the website itself matters for organic rankings and reinforces GBP signals. Most of this is set once at launch — then it's maintenance and expansion.

Title Tags & Meta Descriptions

Title tags are the single most important on-page ranking signal. Every page needs a unique one. Meta descriptions don't directly affect rankings but drive click-through rate.

Page Title Format
Home [Business Name] | [Primary Service] | [City, ST]
Service Page [Service] in [City] | [Business Name]
About About [Business Name] | [City] [Service]
Contact Contact [Business Name] | [City, ST]
  • Keep titles under 60 characters — anything longer gets truncated in search results
  • Meta descriptions: 120–155 characters, include a call to action
  • Every page needs a unique title and description — no duplicates

Schema Markup

Schema is structured data in the page's HTML that tells Google explicitly what the business is, where it's located, and what it does. We ship a LocalBusiness JSON-LD block on every client homepage.

Key fields: name, telephone, address, openingHours, areaServed, url. If a client has a specific niche (e.g., masonry), use the most specific schema type available — HomeAndConstructionBusiness beats generic LocalBusiness.

Validate with Google's Rich Results Test after every launch.

Location Pages

When a client serves multiple cities, create a dedicated page for each one. Each page targets " in " and includes local landmarks, neighborhoods, or testimonials from that area.

Don't build thin location pages

A page that just swaps the city name into a template is worse than nothing — Google may penalize it as duplicate content. Each location page needs at least 200 unique words of genuine local content.

Chapter 6

Client Reporting

Monthly reports are how clients see your value. If they can't see results, they cancel — even when results are real. Learn to tell the story the data is telling.

What to Measure

Metric Source Why It Matters
GBP Views Google Business Profile Insights Shows discovery trend — are more people finding them?
GBP Calls & Direction Requests GBP Insights Direct intent signals — someone took action
Website Sessions GA4 Traffic trend, organic vs direct vs referral
Organic Clicks Google Search Console How many people clicked from Google search
Chatbot Leads Notion CRM / ntfy.sh The most direct ROI metric — leads captured
Review Count & Rating GBP Trust momentum — are reviews growing?

Tools

  • GA4 — Google Analytics 4. Free. Install on every site at launch via a script tag or the Measurement ID.
  • Google Search Console — free, shows keyword impressions and clicks. Verify every site on launch.
  • GBP Insights — built into Google Business Profile dashboard. No setup required.
  • Notion — our CRM where chatbot leads land. Already configured.

Monthly Cadence

  1. Pull GBP Insights for the month (screenshot or export)
  2. Pull GA4 sessions and traffic source breakdown
  3. Pull GSC organic clicks and top queries
  4. Count chatbot leads from Notion for the month
  5. Run report-writer agent with raw data — it formats the report
  6. Send by the 5th of the following month
Frame results, not raw numbers

"Your GBP got 847 views last month — up 23% from October" lands better than "847 views." Always compare to the prior month and note the trend direction.

Chapter 7

Sales & Positioning

Knowing the product is half the job. Knowing how to talk about it — especially when a prospect pushes back — is the other half.

Common Objections

Objection Response
"I already have a website." Ask when it was last updated and whether it has a chatbot. If no to either, you have a clear gap to fill.
"How long until I see results?" Be honest: chatbot works day one. GBP rankings take 60–90 days of consistent work. Set this expectation early.
"Can't I just run ads?" Ads stop the moment you stop paying. A well-ranked GBP and site keep working. Both together is ideal — not either/or.
"That's too expensive." One captured job that would've bounced (missed call, after-hours inquiry) typically pays for 1–2 months. Ask what one average job is worth to them.
"I get most of my work from referrals." Referrals Google the business before calling. If the site looks bad or doesn't exist, the referral converts less often. The site protects the referral flywheel.

Setting Expectations at Onboarding

Most client relationships that sour do so because expectations weren't set clearly at the start. Say these things in writing at onboarding:

  • Timeline: "The chatbot works immediately. GBP rankings take 60–90 days. Organic search can take 3–6 months. We'll show you progress in your monthly report."
  • Your role: "We need job photos from you monthly and access to ask customers for reviews."
  • What we don't control: "We can't guarantee ranking positions — no one honestly can. We control the work that moves the needle."

Upsell Triggers

Watch for these signals that a client is ready to move up a tier or add a service:

  • They're getting leads from the chatbot but saying "I need more" — add a location page or content push
  • They're on Autopilot and asking about social media or GBP posts — offer Momentum
  • They mention a competitor "showing up everywhere" — audit that competitor's GBP and citations, then show the gap
  • They ask about Google Ads — refer out, take a referral fee, keep the relationship

Appendix

Glossary

Definitions for terms used throughout this curriculum and in day-to-day client conversations.

Citation
Any online mention of a business's NAP data — directory listings, social profiles, or any site that references the business name, address, and phone together.
CTA
Call To Action. The action you want a visitor to take — call now, fill out a form, start a chat. Every page should have one clear CTA.
GBP
Google Business Profile. The free Google tool that powers a business's presence in the map pack and on Google Maps. Formerly called Google My Business (GMB).
Impressions
The number of times a search result appeared on screen. Not the same as a click — the user saw it but may not have clicked. Tracked in Google Search Console.
JSON-LD
A format for adding schema markup to a web page. It sits in a <script> tag in the page's <head> and is invisible to users but readable by Google.
Local Pack
The map widget + three business listings that appear near the top of Google search results for local queries. Also called the Map Pack or 3-Pack. High-value placement.
NAP
Name, Address, Phone. These three fields must be identical everywhere a business appears online. Inconsistencies suppress local rankings.
Organic
Non-paid search results. Organic rankings are earned through SEO, not bought through ads. Organic traffic doesn't stop when you stop paying.
Schema / Structured Data
Code added to a webpage that tells search engines explicitly what the page is about. LocalBusiness schema tells Google the business name, address, phone, hours, and service area.
SERP
Search Engine Results Page. The page Google shows after a search. The Local Pack appears near the top of the SERP for local queries, above organic results.
Title Tag
The text shown as the headline of a search result. Also appears in the browser tab. The most important on-page SEO field — every page needs a unique one.
